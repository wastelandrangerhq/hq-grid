Review of Fallout Tactics: Brotherhood of Steel

Dear [Ranger Ben]:

I see on your page that you're requesting a review of Fallout Tactics in terms of its relationship and similarities with Wasteland. I've recently completed the Fallout Tactics game, so I thought I'd send you my comments. Please note that in order to avoid ambiguity in this message, I use the word "genre" to describe RPG vs. tactical, action, etc. and "theme" to discribe post-nuclear vs. fantasy, sci-fi, etc.

First of all, Fallout Tactics is not an RPG. It's a tactical combat game along the lines of Jagged Alliance, XCOM, and Mech Commander in terms of gameplay genre. However, Fallout Tactics does have some RPG-like elements: you do gain experience, which you use to improve your character; and you do collect new and better equipment throughout the game. But that is about all it has in common with any RPG.

Fallout Tactics' tributes to Wasteland are rare. We can only speculate about why this is, but I put forth the following possibilities:

  1. Fallout Tactics is not an RPG
  2. Fallout and Fallout 2 used up all of the similarities in their own tributes to Wasteland
  3. Wasteland is even older now than it was when Fallout came out, so fewer folks would notice tributes to Wasteland. They probably decided to make tributes that more people would notice and understand.
  4. Fallout Tactics was not programmed by the same staff that worked on Fallout and Fallout 2. The new employees at Interplay and the people at 14 Degrees East may have only been vaguely aware of Wasteland and its significance.
There are some similarities between the two games. But it is evident that most of these are coincidental, and have more to do with the switch to a tactical genre than with tributes to Wasteland. These coincidental similarities include:
  1. You are affiliated with a lawkeeping organization in both games.
  2. You have a squad of characters in both games.
  3. You can return to your bunker/HQ to replace dead characters in both games.
The only real similarity that could possibly be a trubite is that in both games, you are fighting against an invasion of robots.

Fallout Tactics was released in March, 2001, and is the third game to make major contributions to the post-nuclear theme in computer games. This, of course, is my own opinion, but here is a list of the three games to make major contributions:

  1. Wasteland: the first [popular] post-nuclear comptuer game.
  2. Fallout: revives the post-nuclear theme, offering modern graphics, features, and gaming technology while retaining the specific post-nuclear ambiance set forth by Wasteland (there are many visions of what a post-nuclear world would be like--Fallout keeps the vision that Wasteland depicted). Fallout 2 was an excellent game, but it didn't make any significant contributions to the post-nuclear theme.
  3. Fallout Tactics: takes post-nuclear computer gaming beyond the RPG, and is the first post-nuclear computer game with multiplayer capabilities.

Please let me know if you'd like any other Fallout Tactics information, and I'll try to help you out.

Best regards,
[T.] Richards
